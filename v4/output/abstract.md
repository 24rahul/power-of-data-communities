# The Power of Open Data: Citation Impact, Research Equity, and Knowledge Diffusion Across Four Major Health Data Repositories

## Abstract

**Background.** Open health data repositories receive billions in public funding, yet no systematic framework exists to evaluate their return on investment in terms of scholarly impact, research equity, or disciplinary reach. We introduce a "citation ripple" methodology that traces both first-degree citations (papers directly using a dataset) and second-degree citations (papers citing those papers) to quantify the full downstream knowledge impact of open data, normalized by funding.

**Methods.** We analyzed four major open health data repositories — MIMIC (versions I–IV; $14.4M funding), UK Biobank ($525.5M), OpenSAFELY ($53.7M), and All of Us ($2,160M) — using the OpenAlex bibliometric database. Citation data were retrieved in February 2026, covering publications through 2025. For each repository, we identified all first-degree citing publications (n = 30,049) and their second-degree citers (n = 485,396). We extracted author demographics (gender via Genderize.io; institutional country via World Bank income classifications), research topics, and citation counts. Impact was normalized per $1M of reported funding. Chi-square tests with Cramér's V and odds ratios assessed equity differences across repositories.

**Results.** Funding-normalized impact varied by orders of magnitude. MIMIC generated 689 first-degree papers and 8,257 second-degree papers per $1M of funding, compared to 1 and 14 respectively for All of Us. Citation ripple amplification was remarkably consistent across repositories (9.3–11.5×), suggesting a universal multiplier effect: every dollar invested in open data generates roughly ten times the scholarly impact beyond its immediate user community. MIMIC produced 141,752 cumulative second-degree citations per $1M, versus 177 for All of Us — an 800-fold difference in downstream knowledge diffusion per dollar.

Author demographics revealed a striking equity–efficiency tension. MIMIC, the most cost-effective repository, had the highest representation of authors from low- and middle-income countries (LMIC; 41.8% vs. 4.3% for All of Us; OR = 17.7, 95% CI 16.5–19.1, p < 0.001) but the lowest female authorship (31.8% vs. 43.2%; OR = 0.61, 95% CI 0.59–0.63, p < 0.001). These disparities persisted across first- and last-author positions. Research from 91–148 countries cited these repositories, with HIC–LMIC collaboration rates ranging from 8.4% to 16.4%.

Disciplinary diffusion analysis revealed that MIMIC is the only repository whose primary research field is computer science (43.1%), not medicine, reflecting its widespread adoption as a machine learning benchmark. UK Biobank research remained concentrated in biochemistry and genetics, while OpenSAFELY was dominated by medicine.

**Conclusions.** Open health data generates a consistent ~10× citation ripple beyond its direct users, but funding efficiency varies 800-fold across repositories when normalized by investment. Smaller, focused repositories like MIMIC achieve outsized impact per dollar and disproportionately serve researchers in low-resource settings, while also seeding research across entirely new scientific disciplines. These findings provide an empirical framework for funders to evaluate open data investments and suggest that modest, well-designed repositories may deliver greater and more equitable scientific returns than large-scale initiatives.

**Keywords:** open data, bibliometrics, citation analysis, research equity, funding efficiency, health data repositories, knowledge diffusion, LMIC
